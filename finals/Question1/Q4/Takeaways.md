My takeaways from the notebook:

The notebook demonstrates that LLMs can be evaluated using comparative, human-centered methods rather than absolute scoring metrics. Instead of measuring correctness directly, models are compared in pairwise “battles,” where human users choose which response they prefer. These outcomes are then converted into numerical skill ratings using the Bradley-Terry model, similar to Elo ratings used in games like chess. The resulting scores are based on probability, meaning higher-rated models are more likely, but not guaranteed to outperform lower-rated ones. The reliability of these rankings depends heavily on the amount of data, as more comparisons lead to more stable and trustworthy results. However, because the evaluation is based on human preferences, it introduces subjectivity, bias, and variability depending on the users and prompts involved. This highlights important limitations in evaluating AI systems, as rankings may not reflect objective correctness but rather perceived usefulness or quality. Overall, the notebook emphasizes the importance of careful experimental design, awareness of bias, and the need for large-scale data when using comparative evaluation methods for AI systems.
